The goal was weight reduction and validating torsional stiffness against an FEA target of 1500 Nm/deg. We removed around 1.5 kg while keeping the suspension kinematic points unchanged.

Constraints

  • Chassis stiffness target (1500 Nm/deg)
  • Maintain suspension pickup points
  • Maintain brake system positioning / mounting
  • Ergonomic requirements
  • Maintain drivetrain placement

Design Phase: Tube Sizing & Optimization

  • Evaluated stiffness to weight ratios for tubing options from VR3
  • Optimized tube selection for a balance of weight and stiffness
  • Simplified the rear structure by removing unnecessary triangulation, reducing mass without compromising integrity
  • Replaced heavy motor controller mounting tubes with a lightweight aluminum plate solution
  • Conducted over 100 finite element analyses (FEA) across varying geometries and tube sizes
  • Performed statistical analysis on chassis weight and torsional stiffness

Testing Setup

The test applies a couple moment at the front axle while the rear is held fixed. Using a couple (two equal and opposite forces) rather than a single static force is deliberate: it applies a pure torque with no net side load, which minimizes the compliance of the test structure itself so the measured displacement reflects the chassis rather than the rig. The moment is increased incrementally and then decreased, and the displacement is measured at each step. We also run the load in reverse to check for hysteresis, confirming the chassis returns along the same path and that the measurements are repeatable. This load-displacement data is then used to calculate the torsional stiffness.

FEA Iteration

For the first set of FEAs we fixed all eight rear A-arm pickup points to simulate our test rig, then applied a couple moment at the front suspension points: four 1100 N forces, which assumes roughly a 1.5 G load on a 330 kg car. This setup is overconstrained and did not give accurate stiffness values, but its short compute time made it useful for quickly comparing one geometry against another.

Preliminary FEA results

This method still did not correlate to our test results, so we added the suspension compliance (the give in the pushrods and A-arms) to the model. Even then, the numbers did not match the test closely enough.

FEA with modelled suspension compliance

I also ran geometry changes one at a time to compare stiffness-to-mass ratios, for example flipping tube orientation or changing tube profile. Isolating one change per run made it clear how each affected the overall design and pointed toward future improvements.

Different geometry matrix

Final FEA Results

The final model used a simplified setup: rather than constraining and loading all four corners, only a single node per corner is constrained or loaded. A fixed support is applied at the right-rear, with the left-rear constrained to allow only lateral deflection, and the load is applied at the front while deflection is measured near the front pickup points. With this setup the FEA returned a torsional stiffness closer to 1000 Nm/deg, which is in line with our physical torsion-test results. It turns out the 1500 Nm/deg goal was carried over from when we were an internal-combustion team, where the engine is bolted in as a stressed member and contributes to chassis stiffness. Without that structural contribution on our electric car, the 1500 Nm/deg target is unrealistic, and ~1000 Nm/deg is a more representative number for our spaceframe.

Design Phase: 2026 Chassis Redesign

I redesigned and personally welded the chassis to house a battery pack 4" smaller in both length and width, reducing wheelbase from 1,575 mm to 1,540 mm (tighter turning radius) and cutting chassis mass by a further 500 g, while still satisfying all FSAE structural and packaging regulations. This required coordinating across subteams:

  • Redesigned to accommodate the smaller battery pack
  • Collaborated with the suspension team on the reduced wheelbase
  • Worked with the aerodynamics team on aero component mounting
  • Coordinated with the drivetrain team on half shaft angle corrections

Manufacturing Phase: Jigging

  • Developed and tested multiple jigging strategies to maintain alignment and dimensional accuracy throughout fabrication
  • Utilized a rigid table setup with clamps and hard stops
  • Adapted jig configurations in critical areas to improve alignment control around key nodes and suspension pickup points
  • Continuously verified diagonals, reference points, and critical dimensions to ensure the chassis remained within tolerance during welding

Manufacturing Phase: 2026 Welding & Fabrication

  • Welded the entire chassis after receiving notched tubes from VR3
  • Carefully managed weld sequencing to minimize heat induced distortion
  • Gained hands on experience in full chassis fabrication, developing a deeper understanding of how manufacturing decisions impact structural performance and final vehicle quality
